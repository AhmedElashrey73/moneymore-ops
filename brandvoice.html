MoneyMore Brand Voice Guide v1.0

Money is stressful.
We aren't.

This is how MoneyMore sounds, what it stands for, and the rules every screen, push, and button label is held to. Read it once, and the next thing you write should sound like us.

01 — Position

What MoneyMore is, and what it isn't.

Before voice, position. Every word we write defends the gap between us and the categories we're often mistaken for. If a sentence could appear in a bank app, a wellness app, or a spreadsheet — rewrite it.

We are

A sharp, warm friend who happens to understand money.

  • Specific. Numbers, categories, real context.
  • Honest about uncertainty, fast to a next step.
  • Confident — we know what we're doing.
  • Calm in moments money usually isn't.
  • Egyptian, fluent, not performing.
We are not

A bank, an advisor, or a wellness app.

  • Not corporate. No "kindly" or "we regret to inform."
  • Not paternal. We don't lecture, judge, or moralise.
  • Not therapeutic. We don't dwell in the stress.
  • Not vague. "You spent a lot lately" is a failure state.
  • Not trying to be cool. Slang we don't own = no.
"You spent 1,240 EGP on food this month" beats
"you've been spending a lot lately."
— The specificity test, applied everywhere
02 — The Five

Five traits that define the voice.

Not adjectives on a slide. Operating instructions. If a piece of copy doesn't carry at least three of these, it isn't ours yet.

— 01

Direct.

Say the thing. No hedging, no corporate softening. If the budget is nearly gone, say it. If the OTP failed, say it clearly and tell the user what to do next.

"Code didn't work. Try again or get a new one."
— 02

Warm but not soft.

Care about the user's financial life without being patronising. Acknowledge that money is stressful — then move toward a solution, not sympathy.

"Slower month than last. Here's what you can still cover."
— 03

Confident, not arrogant.

MoneyMore knows what it's doing. Don't apologise for asking for attention or for action. But never push, never sell.

"Allow notifications and we'll catch overspending before you do."
— 04

Specific over vague.

Numbers, categories, real context. Specificity makes copy feel like it knows the user — because it does. Vague copy reads like every other fintech.

"You're 87% through Food this week. 3 days left."
— 05

Gen Z fluent, not Gen Z performing.

Natural and conversational. Short sentences. Active voice. The occasional sharp turn of phrase. We don't borrow slang we don't own, we don't try to be cool, and we don't reach for a buzzword when a real word exists. Fluency means sounding like a human who happens to be 24 in Cairo — not an app cosplaying as one.

"Salary's in. Want to split it before it splits itself?"
03 — Hard rules

The nevers.

No exceptions, no clever workarounds. If you find yourself wanting to break one of these "just this once," the copy is wrong, not the rule.

We never —

Seven hard lines. Hold them.
  1. Use legal-sounding boilerplate "By proceeding you acknowledge…" — never in user-facing copy. Push it to T&Cs.
  2. Use more than one exclamation per message And only when the moment genuinely earns it. A salary landing earns it. A failed OTP does not.
  3. Use the word "journey" Not your savings journey, not your money journey, not any journey. Banned.
  4. Write in passive voice "Your transaction was declined" → "We couldn't run that. Try a different card."
  5. Judge the user's spending Observe, never moralise. "You spent 800 on takeout this week" — full stop. No "maybe try cooking."
  6. Use filler phrases "Just a reminder," "as you know," "don't forget to" — cut every one of these on sight.
  7. Auto-translate Arabic and ship Conversational Arabic always goes through native speaker review. Always.
04 — In practice

Voice in the wild.

Side-by-side rewrites from the kinds of moments you'll actually be designing for. The "before" is what most fintechs ship. The "after" is us.

SCENARIO 01 — OTP failed during sign-up
Not us

An error has occurred. Please try again or contact support.

Vague, passive, dead-end. User has no idea what to do.

MoneyMore

Code didn't work. Try again, or get a new one.

Plain language, two clear paths forward.

SCENARIO 02 — Empty budgets state, salary worker
Not us

You haven't created any budgets yet. Start your savings journey today!

"Journey" is banned. Two exclamations would be worse. Tells the user nothing.

MoneyMore

No budgets yet. Set one for the category you spend on most — usually food or transport.

Specific next step. Names the likely category. Treats the user as capable.

SCENARIO 03 — Push — overspending alert
Not us

Hey there! Just a friendly reminder that you might want to slow down on spending this week 😊

Filler phrase, hedging, judgmental, performing warmth. Every Gen Z fintech sounds like this.

MoneyMore

Food budget: 87% gone, 3 days left in the week.

Numbers do the work. No moralising. User decides what to do.

SCENARIO 03 — SMS permission prompt
Not us

MoneyMore would like to access your SMS messages to provide you with the best possible experience.

Reads like a privacy policy. A suspicious user taps Deny.

MoneyMore

We read your bank SMS so your transactions show up automatically. No typing, no missed expenses. We never read anything else.

Concrete value. Honest about scope. The reassurance lives in the specificity.

06 — Arabic

Arabic isn't a translation. It's a rewrite.

Egyptian users don't think in Modern Standard Arabic. They text in Egyptian dialect. Our copy follows them — not the other way around.

— Rule 01

Egyptian dialect for conversational copy

Push notifications, surveys, in-app messages, empty states. Anywhere we'd sound like a friend in English, we sound like a friend in Egyptian Arabic.

المرتب نزل. تحب نقسمه قبل ما يقسم نفسه؟
— Rule 02

MSA only for formal copy

Terms & Conditions, legal notices, App Store listing where required. Nowhere else. Formal Arabic in a push notification reads like a government letter.

— Rule 03

Native speaker review, always

If the copy is conversational or uses any colloquial phrasing, it goes through native review before it ships. No exceptions, no "I checked it with a friend."

— Rule 04

RTL confirmed in Figma first

Arabic copy is never finalised until the layout is confirmed RTL in the prototype. Punctuation, numerals, and line breaks behave differently — design and copy lock together.

— Rule 05

Western Arabic numerals

1, 2, 3 — not ١, ٢, ٣. This is the confirmed convention for Egyptian fintech. Mixing systems looks careless and breaks scanning.

— Rule 06

Never auto-translate

Machine translation flattens dialect, breaks gender agreement, and produces copy that sounds technically correct and emotionally wrong. Translate by ear, not by tool.

07 — Channels

The cheat sheet.

Pin this. Every channel has a character ceiling, a structural rule, and a non-negotiable. If a piece of copy doesn't have all three accounted for, it isn't done.

Channel Limit Structural rule Non-negotiable
Push title ≤ 50 chars Lead with the number, the name, or the question One clear action. Always a deep link.
Push body ≤ 100 chars Resolve what the title started. Don't restate it. Tagged to one of 8 notification programmes.
Button label 1–3 words Verb + object. "Set budget" not "Continue." Match the next screen's promise exactly.
Empty state ≤ 2 lines Tell the user what to do — never just describe absence. Always has a CTA or a clear next step.
Error message ≤ 2 lines What happened → why (if useful) → what to do next. Never "something went wrong."
Onboarding screen title + 1–2 lines One idea per screen. The title carries the value. SMS permission screen earns extra care.
Survey question readable in 3s Closed answers. No "I don't know" loopholes. Every answer must produce useful signal.
App Store subtitle ≤ 30 chars Value proposition, not a tagline. Lead the description with the same value in line 1.
08 — Workflow

How a piece of copy gets made.

Designers and writers run this loop together. Skipping a step is how vague, off-brand copy ends up in the build.

Identify the moment.

Channel, segment, and emotional moment. Name what's happening in this user's life right now that makes this message relevant. If you can't name it, the copy isn't ready to be written.

Get the brief or the screen.

Microcopy without screen context is wrong copy. If the request touches a specific surface, the Design Brief or prototype comes first. No exceptions.

Write three variants. Minimum.

Meaningfully different approaches — not the same sentence with different words. One leads with the number. One with the consequence. One with the question. Label them.

Run the checklist.

Character counts. CleverTap event & Mixpanel live status. Segment. Deep link. Notification programme. Arabic native review flag. Anything missing = not shipped.

Surface open questions.

Unresolved threads — RTL confirmation, character limits, conditional logic, missing analytics events — get listed at the end of the deliverable. Never buried inline.
