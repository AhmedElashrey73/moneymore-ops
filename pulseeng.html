What Pulse is — the system, not the pitch

Pulse turns the bank-SMS spend data we already parse into intelligence and campaigns for brand partners. A brand (say a telco) logs in and sees how their category behaves on MoneyMore — their revenue, their customers, where those customers spend elsewhere. Then they run a targeted offer to a slice of those users and we measure whether it actually moved spend.

Architecturally: Pulse is the brain, the MoneyMore app is the surface. Pulse owns all the logic — computing cohorts, resolving audiences, deciding campaigns, measuring results. When a campaign fires, Pulse hands a payload to the app, and the app just renders it (Offers tab + push). No Pulse logic lives inside the app.

Everything rests on two cohorts: the payer cohort (users who pay a given merchant) and the category cohort (where those same users spend everywhere else). We compute these from transaction data — which is exactly where most of my questions are.

The five questions
For each: what I'm asking, why it matters for the build, and what a useful answer sounds like. I don't need perfect answers — I need to know whether each is easy, real work, or unknown. That tells me where the timeline risk sits. Questions 1 and 3 matter most — if we only get through two, those.
1
Layer 0 · Data Foundation HIGHEST PRIORITY
Of all the transaction volume we parse, what % is mapped to a known merchant and category — versus sitting in "Other"? And is coverage even across categories?
Why it matters: share-of-wallet, the category tabs, "top merchants your customers pay" — all of it is only as good as this number. If 40% of spend is "Other," our category breakdowns show less than two-thirds of reality. This caps how impressive the product can look.
A useful answer sounds like"About X% of transaction value is categorised; telecom and food delivery are well-mapped, groceries and small merchants are weak." A non-answer is "most of it" — I'll need a number.
2
Layer 0 · Cohort engine
Can the cohorts and category data run as a scheduled batch job — nightly or weekly — or does anything need to be real-time? Where would real-time actually be required?
Why it matters: batch is dramatically cheaper and simpler. For a monthly-cadence insights product, batch is almost certainly fine — but resolving an audience at the moment a campaign sends might want fresher data. I want to know where the line is.
A useful answer sounds like"Cohorts and insights can be nightly batch; the only thing that might need to be fresher is the audience size at campaign-send time." Push for which parts, not just "it depends."
3
Layer 0 → Layer 3 · Attribution HIGHEST PRIORITY
To measure whether a campaign drove spend — how do we build a fair "control group" of similar users who didn't get the offer, and compare later spend? Have we done matched comparison before, or is it from scratch?
Why it matters: this is the moat. A "+41% lift" claim is only credible if the control group is genuinely comparable. It's statistics, not just a query — the analytical heart of Measure. We should agree the approach on paper before anyone codes it.
A useful answer sounds like"We'd match on prior spend, frequency and recency, hold out a similar group, compare 60-day spend — we have / haven't done matching before." We may not fully resolve this in one sitting; the goal is to know if it's "adapt something" or "design from zero."
4
Layer 2 · The app handoff
When Pulse decides to send a campaign, how should it hand the offer to the app — webhook, shared queue, an API the app polls? And whose time on the app side do we need to build the receiving end?
Why it matters: this is the one cross-team dependency. The integration can be small but still stall if no one on the app side is assigned. I need both the technical shape and the human commitment.
A useful answer sounds like"Pulse posts to an endpoint the app exposes — needs ~X days from whoever owns the Offers tab." If I only get the mechanism and not the person/time, the dependency isn't closed.
5
Cross-cutting · Data location & access
Should Pulse run on the same infrastructure as the consumer app, or be isolated on its own? Given external brand users will log in to see data derived from our users' transactions — what's safest?
Why it matters: external people accessing data derived from our users' bank transactions is a privacy surface the app doesn't have. Isolation usually means a bug in Pulse can't touch live user data — but feasibility is your call.
A useful answer sounds like"Isolated, reading from a derived/copied dataset, so external access never touches the live app DB" — or a reasoned case for shared. I want the reasoning, not just a preference.
What I actually need out of this
For each question: is it easy, real work, or unknown? That alone tells me where the timeline risk sits and lets us start writing build tickets against reality instead of guesses. The categorisation-coverage number (Q1) is the single most valuable thing — it determines how good the product can be before we write a line of code.
