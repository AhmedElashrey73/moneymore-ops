MoneyMore · Sprint Board Guide

One board. Two tracks.
Clear flow left to right.

The board is not chaotic because of the statuses — those are fine. It is chaotic because Fix and Build tickets are mixed together with no visual separation. This guide defines the swim lane structure, who moves tickets and when, and what a healthy sprint board looks like.

Fix Track — bugs and broken experiences
Build Track — new features and improvements
Blocked — stuck, needs a decision
Tool: Jira · Swim lanes by label
Step 1 — the label system

Every ticket gets one label at sprint planning

Labels are how Jira knows which swim lane a ticket belongs to. At sprint planning, before the sprint starts, every ticket gets one of three labels. This takes 30 seconds per ticket and is the engineering lead's responsibility to enforce before the sprint begins.

fix-track
Fix Track
A bug, error, or broken user experience that is live in production right now. Something that exists and is wrong. Examples: FIX-001 budgets 404, FIX-003 OTP failure, any crash surfaced from Crashlytics.
~35% of sprint capacity · Always has priority over Build Track
build-track
Build Track
A new feature, improvement, or capability being built for the first time. Something that does not exist yet. Examples: budget creation redesign, sign-up flow, notification bell, new Offers screen.
~65% of sprint capacity · Can be deprioritised if Fix Track overflows
blocked
Blocked
A ticket that cannot move forward right now — waiting on a decision, a dependency, a design file, or an API spec. Any ticket can become blocked at any point during the sprint. Adding this label surfaces it immediately to everyone.
Must be accompanied by a comment: what is it waiting on, and who owns unblocking it

Step 3 — what each status actually means

Six statuses. Plain language definitions.

These are your existing Jira statuses. The definitions below are what each one actually means for MoneyMore — not the Jira default description. Engineering reads these once and knows exactly when to move a ticket.

To Do
Set by: Engineering lead at sprint planning
Ticket is committed to this sprint and scoped. The assigned engineer has not started yet. All tickets enter the sprint in this status.
No ticket sits in To Do for more than 3 working days. If it does, it is wrongly committed or needs the blocked label.
In Progress
Moved by: Assigned engineer, the moment they start
An engineer is actively working on this ticket. Code is being written or investigation is actively happening. One engineer per ticket — no joint ownership.
Engineers move their own ticket to In Progress the moment they start. Not the next day. Not after standup. The moment they pick it up.
In Review
Moved by: Assigned engineer, when PR is opened
Code is written and a pull request is open waiting for peer review. The engineer is not idle — they can pick up a smaller task while waiting.
PRs must be reviewed within one working day. Sitting in review for 2+ days without comment is a blocker — flag it at next standup.
QA / Validate
Moved by: Engineer after PR is merged to QA environment
Fix or feature is deployed to QA and being tested. Fix Track: verify the bug no longer reproduces. Build Track: functional testing against acceptance criteria.
Ticket must have a comment describing what was tested and the result before it moves to Ready for Production.
Ready for Production
Moved by: Engineering lead after QA sign-off
QA passed. Change is on the internal test build. Waiting for product sign-off before going live. This is the gate product owns — nothing moves to Done without product seeing it here.
Product must respond within one working day. If product does not respond, engineering lead escalates. Silence is not approval.
Done
Moved by: Engineering lead after product sign-off
Change is live in production and confirmed working. For Fix Track, the original bug must be verified fixed in production — not just in QA. Done means users are no longer experiencing the problem.
Fix Track tickets moved to Done must have the Mixpanel event or New Relic signal confirming the fix works in production as a comment.
Step 4 — who does what

Blocked ticket protocol
What to do the moment you are blocked
1
Add the blocked label immediately
Do not wait until standup. The moment forward progress stops, add the label. It surfaces to product and the engineering lead instantly.
2
Add a comment explaining the blocker
One sentence: "Waiting on API endpoint spec from backend" or "Design file for this screen is not ready." Name what is needed and who owns providing it.
3
Ping the right person in Slack
Do not assume the Jira comment will be seen. Send a direct message. Keep Jira as the record, use Slack for speed.
4
Move to another ticket while waiting
A blocked ticket does not mean a blocked engineer. Pick up the next To Do ticket in your track.
5
Remove blocked label the moment it resolves
When the dependency arrives, remove the label and comment: "Unblocked — [what arrived]. Resuming." Ticket returns to its lane automatically.
Step 5 — board updates tied to sprint events

When the board gets updated

Ticket updates are not a separate task — they are tied to existing sprint events. Every sprint event below has a board action attached. If the event happens and the board is not updated, it did not count.

Day 1
Sprint planning
Board actionEng lead labels all tickets. All set to To Do. Swim lanes activated. Capacity split confirmed: 35% Fix / 65% Build.
Day 2
Work begins
Board actionEngineers move their tickets to In Progress as they start. Fix Track moves first.
Day 3
Touchpoint
Board checkEng lead + product review together. Any ticket still in To Do gets flagged. Any blocker addressed.
Fix Track should be In Progress by now. If not — why?
Day 4–5
Sprint work
Board actionEngineers update own tickets as they move. Blocked label added immediately when stuck.
Day 5 signalProduct reviews New Relic + Crashlytics digest in Slack
Day 6–7
Sprint work
Board actionPRs being merged. Fix Track tickets reaching QA. Build Track in review.
Day 8
Touchpoint
Board checkSecond review. Anything not In Review by now is at risk. Scope decisions made here — not on Day 10.
Items that cannot finish move to next sprint backlog now.
Day 9
QA & sign-off
Board actionTickets move QA → Ready for Production. Product reviews same day.
Day 10
Sprint close
Board closeAll items in Done or moved to backlog. Nothing left In Progress or QA when sprint closes.
Sprint review · Retro · Next sprint planning
Quick reference — Day 3 and Day 8 board checks

Healthy board vs problematic board

If you see three or more warning signs at once, the sprint is in trouble. Cut scope now — not on Day 10.

✅
Healthy board
Every ticket has fix-track or build-track label — no unlabelled items
Fix Track and Build Track flow independently in their own lanes
No ticket has been In Progress for more than 3 days without moving
Blocked tickets have a comment explaining what it is waiting on and who owns it
By Day 8, most Fix Track tickets are in QA or Done
Ready for Production items get product sign-off within one working day
Done tickets have a comment confirming the fix works in production
The blocked lane has zero or one item
⚠
Problematic board
Tickets with no label — impossible to know which track they belong to
Fix and Build tickets mixed in the same visual row — no swim lane separation
A ticket In Progress for 4+ days with no comment or update
Blocked ticket with no comment — nobody knows what it is waiting on
On Day 8, Fix Track tickets are still in To Do or In Progress
Ready for Production items sitting 2+ days without product response
Items moved to Done without confirmation they work in production
The blocked lane has 4+ items — the sprint is collapsing
